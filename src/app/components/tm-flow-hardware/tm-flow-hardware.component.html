The methods for assessing the autonomic nervous system within a clinical practice include the evaluation of symptoms and signs, as well as the Heart Rate variability (HRV) analysis, the Cardiac autonomic reflex tests (CART’s), which are based on heart rate and blood pressure recordings. The LD-Oxy System is intended for use in assessing a patient’s SpO2%, Autonomic Nervous System (ANS), and Microvascular Artery Dysfunction by analyzing the pulse waveform (Photoplethysmography - PTG or PPG) provided by the oximeter.

LD-Oxy analyzes the basic rhythms of the NN or RR intervals in Heart Rate Variability (HRV), both in the time domain and the frequency domain, at rest and during challenges (Ewing tests or CARTs), in order to assess a patient’s ANS function and exercise capacity.

LD-Oxy analyses the peripheral microcirculation, both in the time domain (PTG second derivative) and in the frequency or spectral analysis domain (Vasomotion), to assess endothelial function directly without an occlusion test. Vasomotion is defined as the spontaneous rhythmic contraction and dilation of the muscle components in the small blood vessel walls which generate rhythmic changes in their diameter. The LD vasomotion analysis is patented, and in addition to assessing the blood flow, the analysis provides markers of Insulin Resistance (IR), Inflammation (CRP), and Thrombotic risk.

Since the assessment of the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) and the Vascular System require several separate procedures using different diagnostic tools (which can often be expensive, time-consuming and difficult to interpret separately), the TM-Flow System has been developed by integrating various technologies that allow the physician to have a complete overview of the ANS and vascular function with only one exam.

Moreover, the integration of these different technologies reduces the time to perform the different measurements without compromising the accuracy of the results.

Frequently, physicians face patients who have a chronic metabolic disease or other comorbid conditions and symptoms. At times, differentiating vascular from neuropathic disorders can be difficult. The symptoms of autonomic neuropathy and vascular disorders can overlap and mimic each other.

Although the conditions may imitate each other, the causes are often unrelated and treatments are quite different. By integrating technologies, the TM-Flow System allows for the early detection of diabetes and its complications. This will help the physician to distinguish between autonomic neuropathy and vascular dysfunction symptoms. Therefore, TM-Flow offers the physician a tool to better explain the symptom(s) and its cause to the patient, and more treatment options which are easily understood and accepted by the patient.

The TM-Flow System Accuracy

To compare the products‘ accuracy to the Gold standard, we must go to each result of the TM-Flow medical device according to our clinical studies.

1.

CMR Score

At 120 minutes, the correlations between the OGTT (Gold standard for the diabetes diagnostic) and CMRS were: r = 0.56 (p = 0.004) for glucose, r = 0.53 (p = 0.006) for insulin, and r = 0.58 (p = 0.002) for insulin C-peptide. The CMRS had a sensitivity of 92% and specificity of 83% to differentiate diabetic patients and control group patients.

2.

Sudomotor function

The sudomotor response marker NO Peak had a sensitivity of 88% and a specificity of 68% (Area Under the Curve = 0.81, p < 0.0001) to detect retinopathy. The NO Sweat Peak (ESRNO) response marker inversely correlated with lab tests (Gold standard for the microvascular diagnostic) BUN (ρ = −0.41, p < 0.0001), homocysteine (ρ = −0.44, p < 0.0001), fibrinogen (ρ = −0.41, p < 0.0001).

The sudomotor response marker Sweat Peak inversely correlated with the severity of symptoms on the peripheral neuropathy scale (Gold standard for the small fiber neuropathy diagnostic) at ρ = −0.56, p < 0.0001. ESRNO has a specificity and sensitivity of respectively 68.7% and 84.3% to differentiate the Diabetes group and control group.

3.

Cardiac Autonomic Neuropathy

We use the Gold standard and guidelines from the CAN Subcommittee of the Toronto Consensus Panel statement (using Heart rate variability and Ewing tests). Our study showed that the CAN score has a specificity and sensitivity of respectively 90.6% and 74.6% to differentiate the Diabetes group and control group [32] and correlated with CPR at ρ=0.42, p< 0.0001.

4.

Stress Index

Correlated with CRP (Gold standard to detect inflammation) at
ρ=0.40, p< 0.0001.

5.

PTGi

It had a sensitivity of 86.1% and specificity of 87.2% to detect coronary Heart disease using coronary angiography (Gold standard for coronary heart disease diagnostic).

CAD diabetic patients (n=27) had a lower PTGi value compared to the CAD non-diabetic patients (n=38) The mean PTGi in the CAD diabetic patient group was 24 V/s versus 33 V/s in the CAD non-diabetic patient group (P=0.0001). Patients who underwent CABG (n=18) had a higher PTGi value compared to that of the CAD patients who did not undergo CABG surgery (n=47). The mean PTGi for CAD patients who had CABG surgery was 34 V/s versus 30 V/s for CAD patients who did not have CABG surgery (P=0.01).

6.

PTG VLFi

It has a specificity and sensitivity of respectively 96% and 93.6% to differentiate the Diabetes group and control group. It correlated with fibrinogen (the Gold standard for the coagulation disorder diagnostic) at ρ=0.43, p<0.0001.

7.

PTG TP

The ROC curves showed that the most relevant cut-off to the whole study group was a PTG -TP > 406.2. This cut-off had a sensitivity = 95.7%, specificity = 84,4%, and the area under the ROC curve (AUC) = 0.929 for identifying insulin resistance measured with the hyperinsulinemia euglycemic clamp (Gold standard for the insulin resistance diagnostic). All AUC ROC curves analysis was significant (p < 0.0001).

8.

ABIs and SA Amplitudes

Comparison of our results to the Doppler method (Gold standard for the Peripheral artery disease screening) revealed a very good agreement between both methods in terms of clinical relevance: TBL- ABI measurement ≤1 had a sensitivity of 85% and specificity of 96% to detect ABI measured by Doppler ≤0.9 and hence PAD.

9.

Estimated Fat mass

We found adequate relative and absolute agreement between ES-BC and DXA for fat mass (r = 0.97, P < 0.001) with ES-BC overestimating fat mass by 0.1 kg and for body fat percentage (r = 0.92, P < 0.001) with overestimation of fat percentage by 0.4%.

How should the TM-Flow Medical Device be Used in a Clinical Setting?

Our vision is to provide physicians with new tools and a better approach to detect and treat diabetes and its complications. Diabetes is NOT ONLY a blood glucose level concern but also a chronic disease that damages the autonomic nerves and micro/macrovascular systems.

Unfortunately, current laboratory exams offer incomplete markers for the early detection of diabetes and its complications, and for assessing the full effectiveness of treatment. In adjunct with the laboratory tests, our ANS testing medical device platform aims to provide better solutions to improve patient outcomes that will benefit the overall care of patients.
